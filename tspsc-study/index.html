1. RCC Design (IS 456) - Highest Weightage

Exam Tip: 20-25 questions expected. Focus on limit state design, xu/d values, beam/slab/column design, development length. 4-5 numericals every year.

1.1 Limit State Method

Q1. What is the partial safety factor for concrete in limit state of collapse?
Answer: 1.5
Explanation: As per IS 456:2000, Clause 36.4.2, the partial safety factor for concrete is 1.5. This means design strength of concrete = 0.67fck/1.5 = 0.447fck. The 0.67 accounts for difference between cube strength and actual strength in structure. The 1.5 covers material variability. Steel has lower factor (1.15) because it is manufactured under controlled conditions.
Q2. What is the partial safety factor for steel?
Answer: 1.15
Explanation: Design strength of steel = fy/1.15 = 0.87fy. This is why 0.87fy appears in all RCC formulas. Steel gets a lower safety factor than concrete because steel production is factory-controlled with less variability in strength compared to concrete which is mixed on-site.
Q3. What is the maximum strain in concrete at failure?
Answer: 0.0035 (at extreme compression fiber)
Explanation: IS 456 assumes concrete fails at strain of 0.0035 at the extreme compression fiber. The stress-strain curve is parabolic from 0 to 0.002 strain (where stress = 0.447fck), then constant from 0.002 to 0.0035. This is the basis of the rectangular-parabolic stress block used in limit state design.
Q4. What is xu,max/d for Fe415, Fe500, and Fe250?
Answer: Fe415 = 0.48, Fe500 = 0.46, Fe250 = 0.53
Explanation: xu,max/d is the neutral axis depth ratio for a balanced section. It is derived from strain compatibility:

At balanced failure: concrete strain at top = 0.0035, steel strain at bottom = 0.002 + 0.87fy/Es

Using similar triangles: xu/(d-xu) = 0.0035/(strain in steel)

For Fe415: steel strain = 0.002 + 0.87x415/200000 = 0.0038
xu/d = 0.0035/(0.0035+0.0038) = 0.48

For Fe500: steel strain = 0.002 + 0.87x500/200000 = 0.00417
xu/d = 0.0035/(0.0035+0.00417) = 0.46

If xu > xu,max: over-reinforced (not allowed). If xu < xu,max: under-reinforced (preferred).
xu,max/d = 0.0035 / (0.0035 + 0.002 + 0.87fy/Es)
Q5. What is Mu,lim for Fe415?
Answer: Mu,lim = 0.138 fck.b.d²
Explanation: Moment of resistance of balanced section:
Mu = 0.36.fck.b.xu(d - 0.42xu)

For Fe415, xu,max = 0.48d:
Mu,lim = 0.36 x fck x b x 0.48d x (d - 0.42x0.48d)
= 0.36 x 0.48 x (1 - 0.2016) x fck.b.d²
= 0.36 x 0.48 x 0.7984 x fck.b.d²
= 0.138 fck.b.d²

Similarly: Fe500 = 0.133fck.bd², Fe250 = 0.149fck.bd²

If required Mu > Mu,lim, design as doubly reinforced beam.
Q6. What is minimum tension reinforcement in beams?
Answer: As,min = 0.85bd/fy
Explanation: IS 456, Clause 26.5.1.1. Purpose: prevent sudden brittle failure when concrete cracks. Without minimum steel, the moment capacity of cracked section would be less than uncracked section, causing instant failure.

For Fe415: As,min = 0.85bd/415 = 0.205% of bd
For Fe500: As,min = 0.85bd/500 = 0.17% of bd
Q7. What is maximum spacing of shear reinforcement?
Answer: 0.75d or 300mm, whichever is less
Explanation: IS 456, Clause 26.5.1.6. This ensures every diagonal shear crack (at ~45°) is crossed by at least one stirrup. If spacing > 0.75d, a crack could pass between two stirrups without being resisted.

Minimum shear reinforcement spacing: Sv = 0.87fy.Asv/(0.4b)
This provides minimum shear resistance even where calculated shear is low.

1.2 Slabs

Q8. When is a slab one-way vs two-way?
Answer: One-way: Ly/Lx > 2, Two-way: Ly/Lx • 2
Explanation: When longer span is more than twice the shorter span, the slab bends primarily in one direction (shorter span). Load transfer is one-way.

One-way slab: Main steel along shorter span, distribution steel (0.12% for HYSD) along longer span.
Two-way slab: Main steel in both directions, BM coefficients from IS 456 Table 26 based on edge conditions.
Q9. What are span/depth ratios for deflection control?
Answer: Simply supported = 20, Continuous = 26, Cantilever = 7
Explanation: IS 456, Clause 23.2.1. These basic values ensure deflection < L/250 (total) and L/350 (after partitions). Modified by:
1. Tension reinforcement factor (Fig. 4) - reduces ratio for high steel %
2. Compression reinforcement factor (Fig. 5) - increases ratio
3. Flanged beams: multiply by 0.8
4. Span > 10m: multiply by 10/span

1.3 Columns

Q10. Short column condition and load capacity?
Answer: Short when Le/D • 12. Pu = 0.4fck.Ac + 0.67fy.Asc
Explanation: Short column (Le/D • 12): fails by crushing, no buckling effect.
Long column (Le/D > 12): additional moment due to lateral deflection (P-delta).

Load capacity: Pu = 0.4fck(Ag-Asc) + 0.67fy.Asc
The 0.4 = 0.447 x 0.9 (accounts for accidental eccentricity)
The 0.67 = 0.87 x 0.77 (steel doesn't reach full yield in compression)

Reinforcement limits: Min = 0.8%, Max = 6% (practical 4%)
Ties spacing: least of (least dimension, 16d, 300mm)

1.4 Development Length & Footings

Q11. What is development length and its formula?
Answer: Ld = 0.87fy.x? / (4.x"bd)
Explanation: Development length is the minimum length of bar required to develop full tensile/compressive strength through bond with concrete.

Ld = x?.xfs/(4.x"bd) = 0.87fy.x?/(4.x"bd)

For Fe415 in M20: x"bd = 1.2 N/mm² (plain bars), increased by 60% for deformed bars = 1.92 N/mm²
Ld = 0.87 x 415 x x? / (4 x 1.92) = 47x?

So for 16mm bar: Ld = 47 x 16 = 752mm x?^ 750mm

Anchorage value of 90° bend = 8x?, 180° hook = 16x?
Fe415 in M20: Ld = 47x? | Fe415 in M25: Ld = 40x? | Fe500 in M20: Ld = 57x?
Q12. Where is punching shear and one-way shear checked in footings?
Answer: Punching shear at d/2 from column face. One-way shear at d from column face.
Explanation: In isolated footings:

1. Punching shear (two-way shear): Checked on a perimeter at d/2 from column face. The critical section forms a rectangle/square around the column. Punching shear stress = Vu/(perimeter x d). Allowable = ks x 0.25x^sfck.

2. One-way shear: Checked at section d from column face (like a beam). Compare Vu/bd with x"c from IS 456 Table 19.

3. Bending moment: Calculated at face of column (not at d from face).
Q13. What are the nominal cover requirements?
Answer: Beam = 25mm, Column = 40mm, Footing = 50mm, Slab = 15-20mm
Explanation: IS 456, Table 16 & 16A. Cover protects reinforcement from:
1. Corrosion (environmental exposure)
2. Fire (insulation)
3. Proper bond development

Cover depends on exposure condition:
Mild: 20mm (slab), 25mm (beam)
Moderate: 30mm
Severe: 45mm
Very severe: 50mm
Extreme: 75mm

Footing always gets 50mm (in contact with soil).

2. Strength of Materials - 18-22 Questions Expected

Exam Tip: Second highest weightage. Focus on bending equation, deflection formulas, torsion, columns (Euler), thin cylinders, and Mohr's circle. Memorize all deflection formulas!

2.1 Stress, Strain & Elastic Constants

Q14. What is the relationship between E, G, and μ?
Answer: E = 2G(1 + μ)
Explanation: This relates Young's modulus (E), Shear modulus (G), and Poisson's ratio (μ). For steel: E = 200 GPa, G = 80 GPa.
Verification: 200 = 2 x 80 x (1 + μ) -> μ = 0.25 xo"

Other relationships:
• E = 3K(1 - 2μ) x?" relates E with Bulk modulus K
• E = 9KG/(3K + G) x?" relates all three

For incompressible material: μ = 0.5 (maximum possible value)
For most metals: μ = 0.25 to 0.35
For concrete: μ = 0.15 to 0.20
E = 2G(1+μ) = 3K(1-2μ) = 9KG/(3K+G)
Q15. What is the elongation of a bar under axial load?
Answer: δ = PL/AE
Explanation: From Hooke's law: xf = Eε -> P/A = E x (δ/L) -> δ = PL/AE

Key variations:
• Bar with varying sections: δ = P/E x (Lx,x/Ax,x + Lx,,/Ax,, + Lx,f/Ax,f)
• Tapered circular bar (dx,x to dx,,): δ = 4PL/(x?Edx,xdx,,)
• Bar under self-weight: δ = WL/2AE = ρgL²/2E (W = total weight)
• Conical bar under self-weight: δ = ρgL²/6E (1/3 of uniform bar)

Strain energy stored: U = P²L/2AE = xf²V/2E
δ = PL/AE | Tapered: δ = 4PL/(x?Edx,xdx,,) | Self-weight: δ = ρgL²/2E
Q16. Stress due to suddenly applied load vs gradually applied load?
Answer: Sudden load produces 2x the stress of gradual load
Explanation:
• Gradually applied: xf = P/A (load increases from 0 to P slowly)
• Suddenly applied: xf = 2P/A (load applied at full value instantly)
• Impact load (dropped from height h): xf = (P/A)[1 + x^s(1 + 2h/δst)]

Where δst = PL/AE (static deflection)

For h = 0 (sudden): xf = (P/A)[1 + x^s(1+0)] = 2P/A xo"
For large h: xf x?^ (P/A)x^s(2h/δst) = x^s(2PEh/AL)
Q17. What is thermal stress?
Answer: xf = Eαx"T (when bar is fixed at both ends)
Explanation: When a bar is free to expand: no stress, only strain = αx"T
When fixed at both ends: no strain allowed, so stress develops.

xf = E x α x x"T

Where α = coefficient of thermal expansion (for steel x?^ 12 x 10⁻⁶/°C)

Temperature rise -> compressive stress (bar wants to expand but can't)
Temperature drop -> tensile stress (bar wants to contract but can't)

If partially restrained (spring support): xf = Eαx"T x k/(k + AE/L)

2.2 Shear Force & Bending Moment

Q18. What are the key BM formulas for standard beams?
Answer: SS+central load: PL/4 | SS+UDL: wL²/8 | Cantilever+end load: PL | Cantilever+UDL: wL²/2
Explanation: Most frequently asked formulas:

Simply Supported Beam:
• Central point load P: Mmax = PL/4 (at center), Vmax = P/2
• UDL w/m: Mmax = wL²/8 (at center), Vmax = wL/2
• Eccentric load at 'a' from left: Mmax = Pab/L (at load point)

Cantilever Beam:
• Point load P at free end: Mmax = PL (at fixed end), V = P
• UDL w/m: Mmax = wL²/2 (at fixed end), Vmax = wL

Fixed Beam:
• Central load: FEM = PL/8, mid-span M = PL/8
• UDL: FEM = wL²/12, mid-span M = wL²/24

Key relationship: dM/dx = V (shear force = slope of BM diagram)
SS central: wL²/8 | Cantilever UDL: wL²/2 | Fixed UDL: wL²/12 (support), wL²/24 (midspan)
Q19. What is point of contraflexure?
Answer: Point where bending moment changes sign (BM = 0)
Explanation: At point of contraflexure:
• BM = 0 (changes from sagging to hogging or vice versa)
• Curvature changes direction
• It exists only in beams with both sagging and hogging moments (continuous beams, propped cantilevers, fixed beams)

Simply supported beam with UDL: NO point of contraflexure (BM is always positive)
Fixed beam with UDL: 2 points of contraflexure at 0.211L from each support
Propped cantilever with UDL: 1 point at L/4 from propped end

2.3 Bending & Shear Stresses

Q20. What is the bending equation?
Answer: M/I = xf/y = E/R
Explanation: This is the fundamental equation of bending:
• M = bending moment at section
• I = moment of inertia about neutral axis
• xf = bending stress at distance y from NA
• y = distance from neutral axis
• E = Young's modulus
• R = radius of curvature

Key points:
• xf is maximum at extreme fibers (y = ymax)
• xf = 0 at neutral axis
• xfmax = M/Z where Z = I/ymax (section modulus)
• Z for rectangle = bd²/6
• Z for circle = x?d³/32
• NA passes through centroid of cross-section
xfmax = M/Z | Z(rect) = bd²/6 | Z(circle) = x?d³/32 | I(rect) = bd³/12
Q21. What is maximum shear stress in rectangular and circular sections?
Answer: Rectangular: x"max = 1.5(V/A) | Circular: x"max = 4V/3A
Explanation: Shear stress formula: x" = VQ/(Ib)

Rectangular section:
x" varies parabolically, zero at top/bottom, maximum at NA
x"max = 1.5 x x"avg = 1.5 x V/(bd)

Circular section:
x"max = (4/3) x x"avg = 4V/(3A) = 4V/(3xx?d²/4)

I-section:
x"max at NA (in web), sudden jump at flange-web junction
Most of shear is carried by web (x?^90-95%)

Triangular section:
x"max = 3V/2A (at mid-height, NOT at NA)
x" at NA = 8V/3bh

2.5 Torsion

Q23. What is the torsion equation?
Answer: T/J = x"/r = Gθ/L
Explanation:
• T = torque/twisting moment
• J = polar moment of inertia
• x" = shear stress at radius r
• r = radial distance from center
• G = modulus of rigidity (shear modulus)
• θ = angle of twist
• L = length of shaft

Key formulas:
• J (solid) = x?d⁴/32
• J (hollow) = x?(D⁴-d⁴)/32
• x"max (solid) = 16T/x?d³ (at outer surface)
• Power: P = 2x?NT/60 (N in rpm, T in N·m, P in watts)
• Angle of twist: θ = TL/GJ

Shear stress is zero at center, maximum at outer surface (linear variation).
x"max = 16T/x?d³ | J = x?d⁴/32 | θ = TL/GJ | P = 2x?NT/60

2.6 Columns (Euler's Theory)

Q24. What is Euler's critical load and effective lengths?
Answer: Pcr = x?²EI/Le² | Effective lengths: Pin-Pin=L, Fix-Fix=0.5L, Fix-Free=2L, Fix-Pin=0.7L
Explanation: Euler's formula gives the buckling load for long columns:

Pcr = x?²EI/Le²

Effective length Le depends on end conditions:
• Both ends pinned: Le = L (factor = 1.0)
• Both ends fixed: Le = 0.5L (factor = 0.5) x?" strongest
• One fixed, one free: Le = 2L (factor = 2.0) x?" weakest
• One fixed, one pinned: Le = 0.7L (factor = 0.7)

Euler's formula is valid only for long columns (slenderness ratio > critical value x?^ 80 for steel).
For short columns: use Rankine's formula: 1/P = 1/Pc + 1/Pe

Slenderness ratio = Le/rmin where r = x^s(I/A) = radius of gyration
Pcr = x?²EI/Le² | Le: Pin-Pin=L, Fix-Fix=L/2, Fix-Free=2L, Fix-Pin=0.7L

2.7 Thin Cylinders

Q25. What are hoop stress and longitudinal stress in thin cylinders?
Answer: Hoop (circumferential) = pd/2t | Longitudinal = pd/4t | Ratio = 2:1
Explanation: For thin-walled pressure vessels (t/d < 1/20):

Hoop stress (xfh): Acts circumferentially, tends to split cylinder along length
xfh = pd/2t (p = internal pressure, d = diameter, t = thickness)

Longitudinal stress (xfl): Acts along axis, tends to separate end caps
xfl = pd/4t

xfh = 2xfl -> Hoop stress is TWICE longitudinal stress
-> Cylinder fails along longitudinal joint (due to higher hoop stress)
-> Riveted cylinders need stronger longitudinal joints

Thin sphere: xf = pd/4t (equal in all directions)

Volumetric strain: εv = (pd/4tE)(5 - 4μ) for cylinder
Volumetric strain: εv = (3pd/4tE)(1 - μ) for sphere
xfhoop = pd/2t | xflong = pd/4t | xfhoop/xflong = 2 | Sphere: xf = pd/4t

2.8 Moment Distribution & Structural Analysis Basics

Q26. What is carry-over factor and stiffness?
Answer: COF = 1/2 (far end fixed), 0 (far end pinned) | Stiffness = 4EI/L (fixed), 3EI/L (pinned)
Explanation: In moment distribution method:

Stiffness (k): Moment required to produce unit rotation at near end
• Far end fixed: k = 4EI/L
• Far end pinned: k = 3EI/L (= 3/4 of fixed end stiffness)
• Far end free: k = 0

Distribution Factor (DF): DF = k/(Σk) at a joint
Sum of all DFs at a joint = 1.0
Fixed support: DF = 0 (no distribution)

Carry-Over Factor (COF):
• Far end fixed: COF = +1/2 (half the moment carries over, same sign)
• Far end pinned: COF = 0 (nothing carries over)

Fixed End Moments (FEM):
• UDL: ±wL²/12
• Central point load: ±PL/8
• Eccentric load: MAB = Pab²/L², MBA = Pa²b/L²
k(fixed) = 4EI/L | k(pinned) = 3EI/L | COF = 1/2 | FEM(UDL) = wL²/12 | FEM(central P) = PL/8
Q27. What is the shape factor for rectangular and circular sections?
Answer: Rectangular = 1.5, Circular = 1.7 (16/3x?), I-section x?^ 1.12-1.15
Explanation: Shape factor = Zp/Ze = Mp/My (plastic moment / yield moment)

• Zp (plastic section modulus) = moment when entire section yields
• Ze (elastic section modulus) = I/ymax

Rectangular: Zp = bd²/4, Ze = bd²/6 -> SF = (bd²/4)/(bd²/6) = 1.5
Circular: Zp = d³/6, Ze = x?d³/32 -> SF = (d³/6)/(x?d³/32) = 16/3x? = 1.7
I-section: SF x?^ 1.12-1.15 (most material at flanges, already near yield)
Diamond: SF = 2.0

Higher shape factor = more reserve strength beyond first yield.
Collapse load = Shape factor x First yield load

3. Fluid Mechanics & Hydraulics - 15-18 Questions Expected

Exam Tip: Focus on Bernoulli's applications, pipe flow (Darcy), open channel (Manning), hydraulic jump, and dimensional analysis. Reynolds number and Froude number are asked every year.

3.1 Fluid Properties & Pressure

Q28. What is Reynolds number and its significance?
Answer: Re = ρVD/μ = VD/ν | Laminar < 2000 | Turbulent > 4000
Explanation: Reynolds number = Inertia force / Viscous force

Re = ρVD/μ = VD/ν
Where: ρ = density, V = velocity, D = diameter, μ = dynamic viscosity, ν = kinematic viscosity

For pipe flow:
• Re < 2000: Laminar flow (viscous forces dominate, orderly layers)
• 2000 < Re < 4000: Transition zone (unstable)
• Re > 4000: Turbulent flow (inertia forces dominate, chaotic mixing)

For open channel flow: Re based on hydraulic radius R
• Laminar: Re < 500
• Turbulent: Re > 1000

For flat plate: Re based on distance x from leading edge
• Laminar: Rex < 5x10⁵
• Turbulent: Rex > 5x10⁵
Q29. State and explain Bernoulli's equation.
Answer: P/ρg + V²/2g + z = constant (along a streamline)
Explanation: Bernoulli's equation is conservation of energy for ideal fluid:

P/ρg + V²/2g + z = constant

• P/ρg = pressure head (energy due to pressure)
• V²/2g = velocity head (kinetic energy)
• z = elevation head (potential energy)

Assumptions:
1. Steady flow
2. Incompressible fluid
3. Inviscid (no friction)
4. Along a streamline
5. No energy addition/removal

Applications:
• Venturimeter: Q = Cd x Ax,xAx,,x^s(2gh) / x^s(Ax,x²-Ax,,²)
• Pitot tube: V = Cvx^s(2gh)
• Orifice: V = Cvx^s(2gh), Q = Cdx-Ax-x^s(2gh)

With losses: Px,x/ρg + Vx,x²/2g + zx,x = Px,,/ρg + Vx,,²/2g + zx,, + hL
Q30. What is Darcy-Weisbach equation for pipe flow?
Answer: hf = fLV²/(2gD)
Explanation: Head loss due to friction in pipes:

hf = fLV²/(2gD) = fLQ²/(12.1 x D⁵) [for Q in m³/s]

Where: f = Darcy friction factor, L = pipe length, V = velocity, D = diameter

Friction factor:
• Laminar flow: f = 64/Re (Hagen-Poiseuille)
• Turbulent smooth: 1/x^sf = 2log(Rex^sf) - 0.8 (Colebrook)
• Turbulent rough: f from Moody diagram

Laminar flow in pipe:
• Velocity profile: parabolic
• Vmax = 2xVavg (at center)
• Q = x?d⁴x"P/(128μL) (Hagen-Poiseuille)
• x"wall = 8μVavg/R

Minor losses: hL = KV²/2g
• Sudden expansion: K = (1-Ax,x/Ax,,)², hL = (Vx,x-Vx,,)²/2g
• Sudden contraction: K = 0.5(1-Ax,,/Ax,x)
• Entry: K = 0.5, Exit: K = 1.0
hf = fLV²/2gD | f(laminar) = 64/Re | Vmax = 2Vavg (laminar) | Q = x?d⁴x"P/128μL

3.2 Open Channel Flow

Q31. What is Manning's equation and most economical sections?
Answer: V = (1/n)R²/³S¹/² | Best rectangular: b=2d | Best trapezoidal: half-hexagon
Explanation: Manning's equation for uniform flow in open channels:

V = (1/n) x R^(2/3) x S^(1/2)
Q = A x V = (A/n) x R^(2/3) x S^(1/2)

Where: n = Manning's roughness coefficient, R = A/P (hydraulic radius), S = bed slope

Typical n values: concrete = 0.013, earth = 0.025, natural stream = 0.035

Most economical sections (maximum Q for given A):
• Rectangular: b = 2d (width = 2xdepth), R = d/2
• Trapezoidal: R = d/2, half-hexagon shape (side slope = 60°)
• Circular: depth = 0.81D for max velocity, 0.95D for max discharge
• Of all shapes: semicircle is most efficient (minimum perimeter for given area)

Chezy's equation: V = Cx^s(RS), where C = (1/n)R^(1/6) (Manning-Chezy relation)
Q32. What is critical flow and Froude number?
Answer: Fr = V/x^s(gy) | Critical: Fr=1 | Subcritical: Fr<1 | Supercritical: Fr>1
Explanation: Froude number = Inertia force / Gravity force = V/x^s(gD)
For rectangular channel: D = y (depth), so Fr = V/x^s(gy)

Flow classification:
• Fr < 1: Subcritical (tranquil) x?" deep, slow, controlled from downstream
• Fr = 1: Critical flow x?" specific energy is minimum
• Fr > 1: Supercritical (rapid/shooting) x?" shallow, fast, controlled from upstream

Critical depth (rectangular):
yc = (q²/g)^(1/3) where q = Q/b (discharge per unit width)
Vc = x^s(gyc)
Ec = 1.5yc (critical specific energy = 1.5 x critical depth)

Hydraulic jump: Supercritical -> Subcritical (energy dissipation)
yx,,/yx,x = ½[x^s(1+8Frx,x²) - 1] (sequent depth ratio)
Energy loss: x"E = (yx,,-yx,x)³/(4yx,xyx,,)
yc = (q²/g)^(1/3) | Ec = 1.5yc | Jump: yx,,/yx,x = ½[x^s(1+8Frx,x²)-1] | x"E = (yx,,-yx,x)³/4yx,xyx,,

4. Soil Mechanics & Foundation Engineering - 15-18 Questions Expected

Exam Tip: Focus on index properties (Se=wGs), permeability, consolidation (Tv values), shear strength, Terzaghi bearing capacity, and Rankine earth pressure. Very scoring if formulas are memorized!

4.1 Index Properties & Classification

Q33. What are the fundamental phase relationships in soil?
Answer: Se = wGs | γd = γ/(1+w) | n = e/(1+e) | γsat = (Gs+e)γw/(1+e)
Explanation: Soil is a 3-phase system (solid + water + air):

Key relationships:
• Void ratio: e = Vv/Vs (can be > 1)
• Porosity: n = Vv/V = e/(1+e) (always < 1)
• Water content: w = Ww/Ws (can be > 100%)
• Degree of saturation: S = Vw/Vv (0 to 1)
• Se = wGs (most important relationship!)

Density formulas:
• Bulk density: γ = Gs(1+w)γw/(1+e)
• Dry density: γd = γ/(1+w) = Gsγw/(1+e)
• Saturated (S=1): γsat = (Gs+e)γw/(1+e)
• Submerged: γ' = γsat - γw = (Gs-1)γw/(1+e)

Typical values: Gs = 2.65 (sand), 2.70 (clay), γw = 9.81 kN/m³

Bligh's Creep Theory (1910):
• Water creeps along the contact surface of structure and soil
• Creep length L must be • Cx-H (C = creep coefficient, H = head)
• C values: Fine sand=15, Coarse sand=12, Gravel=5-9
• Hydraulic gradient: i = H/L • 1/C (safe)
• Limitation: doesn't distinguish between horizontal and vertical creep

Khosla's Theory (1936):
• Based on flow net analysis and potential theory
• Considers actual pressure distribution under structure
• Uses method of independent variables (each element analyzed separately)
• Exit gradient: GE = H/(dx-x?x^sλ) where λ = 1+x^s(1+α²)/2, α = b/d
• Safe exit gradient: 1/5 to 1/6 for sand
• Corrections: thickness, mutual interference, slope

Khosla's theory is more rational and widely used for modern weir/barrage design.

11.1 Springs

Q65. What are the formulas for close-coiled helical springs?
Answer: Deflection delta = 64WR³n/(Gd^4) | Shear stress tau = 16WR/(pi.d³) x Ks (Wahl factor)
Explanation: For close-coiled helical spring under axial load W:

Shear stress (without correction):
tau = 16WR/(pi.d³)

With Wahl's correction factor Ks:
tau = Ks x 16WR/(pi.d³)
Ks = (4C-1)/(4C-4) + 0.615/C
Where C = D/d = spring index (ratio of mean coil diameter to wire diameter)
Typical C = 4 to 12 (ideal: 6-9)

Deflection:
delta = 64WR³n/(Gd^4) = 8WD³n/(Gd^4)
Where: n = number of active coils, G = modulus of rigidity

Stiffness (spring rate):
k = W/delta = Gd^4/(64R³n)

Springs in parallel: keq = k1 + k2 (same deflection, loads add)
Springs in series: 1/keq = 1/k1 + 1/k2 (same load, deflections add)

Strain energy: U = W² x 64R³n/(2Gd^4)
delta = 64WR³n/(Gd^4) | tau = Ks x 16WR/(pi.d³) | k = Gd^4/(64R³n) | C = D/d
Q66. Two springs of stiffness 10 kN/m and 15 kN/m are connected (a) in series, (b) in parallel. Find equivalent stiffness and deflection under 5 kN load.
Answer: Series: keq = 6 kN/m, delta = 0.833m | Parallel: keq = 25 kN/m, delta = 0.2m
Explanation:

(a) Springs in Series:
Same force through both, deflections add up.
1/keq = 1/k1 + 1/k2 = 1/10 + 1/15 = 5/30
keq = 6 kN/m
delta = W/keq = 5/6 = 0.833 m

Individual: delta1 = 5/10 = 0.5m, delta2 = 5/15 = 0.333m. Total = 0.833m

(b) Springs in Parallel:
Same deflection, forces add up.
keq = k1 + k2 = 10 + 15 = 25 kN/m
delta = W/keq = 5/25 = 0.2 m

Key insight: Series = softer (less stiff), Parallel = stiffer.
Series: 1/keq = 1/k1+1/k2 | Parallel: keq = k1+k2 | delta = W/k

11.2 Mohr's Circle

Q67. At a point: sigma_x = 80 MPa (T), sigma_y = 40 MPa (C), tau_xy = 30 MPa. Find principal stresses and max shear stress.
Answer: sigma1 = 87.1 MPa, sigma2 = -47.1 MPa, tau_max = 67.1 MPa, theta_p = 13.3 deg
Explanation:

Given: sigma_x = +80 MPa, sigma_y = -40 MPa, tau_xy = 30 MPa

Principal stresses formula:
sigma_1,2 = (sigma_x+sigma_y)/2 +/- sqrt[(sigma_x-sigma_y)²/4 + tau_xy²]

Step 1: Average stress = (80+(-40))/2 = 20 MPa

Step 2: Radius of Mohr's circle:
R = sqrt[(80-(-40))²/4 + 30²] = sqrt[3600 + 900] = sqrt(4500) = 67.08 MPa

Step 3: Principal stresses:
sigma1 = 20 + 67.08 = 87.08 MPa (tensile)
sigma2 = 20 - 67.08 = -47.08 MPa (compressive)

Step 4: Maximum shear stress:
tau_max = R = 67.08 MPa (at 45 deg to principal planes)

Step 5: Principal plane angle:
tan(2.theta_p) = 2.tau_xy/(sigma_x-sigma_y) = 60/120 = 0.5
2.theta_p = 26.57 deg, theta_p = 13.28 deg
sigma_1,2 = (sx+sy)/2 +/- sqrt[(sx-sy)²/4 + tau²] | tau_max = R | tan2theta = 2tau/(sx-sy)
Q68. A shaft has bending moment M=10 kN.m and torque T=8 kN.m simultaneously. Find equivalent bending moment and equivalent torque.
Answer: Me = 11.4 kN.m | Te = 12.81 kN.m
Explanation: Combined bending and torsion on shaft:

Equivalent Bending Moment (max normal stress theory):
Me = 0.5[M + sqrt(M² + T²)]
= 0.5[10 + sqrt(100 + 64)]
= 0.5[10 + 12.81] = 11.4 kN.m

Equivalent Torque (max shear stress theory):
Te = sqrt(M² + T²) = sqrt(164) = 12.81 kN.m

Design using:
sigma_max = 32Me/(pi.d³) <= sigma_allow
tau_max = 16Te/(pi.d³) <= tau_allow

Max shear stress theory (Guest's) is preferred for ductile materials.
Me = 0.5[M+sqrt(M²+T²)] | Te = sqrt(M²+T²) | sigma = 32Me/pi.d³ | tau = 16Te/pi.d³
Q69. A cylindrical vessel: d=1.5m, t=12mm, internal pressure p=2 MPa. Find principal stresses and max shear stress.
Answer: Hoop = 125 MPa, Longitudinal = 62.5 MPa, tau_max = 62.5 MPa
Explanation:

Given: d = 1500mm, t = 12mm, p = 2 MPa. Check: t/d = 0.008 < 1/20 (thin cylinder)

Hoop stress: sigma_h = pd/(2t) = 2x1500/(2x12) = 125 MPa
Longitudinal stress: sigma_l = pd/(4t) = 2x1500/(4x12) = 62.5 MPa

sigma_h = 2 x sigma_l (hoop is TWICE longitudinal)
Cylinder fails along longitudinal joint (due to higher hoop stress)

Max in-plane shear: (sigma_h - sigma_l)/2 = 31.25 MPa
Absolute max shear: sigma_h/2 = 62.5 MPa (considering sigma3=0)

Volumetric strain: ev = (pd/4tE)(5-4mu)
For sphere: sigma = pd/4t (equal in all directions)
sigma_h = pd/2t | sigma_l = pd/4t | sigma_h/sigma_l = 2 | Sphere: sigma = pd/4t

Q72. What are the hydraulic coefficients for an orifice?
Answer: Cd = 0.62, Cv = 0.98, Cc = 0.64 | Cd = Cv x Cc
Explanation:

1. Coefficient of Velocity (Cv):
Cv = Actual velocity / Theoretical velocity = V_actual / sqrt(2gH)
Typical: 0.95 to 0.99 (approx 0.98)

Cc = Area at vena contracta / Area of orifice
Typical: 0.62 to 0.65 (approx 0.64)

Cd = Cv x Cc. Typical: 0.60 to 0.65 (approx 0.62)

t = (2A)/(Cd.a.sqrt(2g)) x [sqrt(H1) - sqrt(H2)]

Q74. What are the key formulas for centrifugal pumps?

13.1 Standard Penetration Test (SPT)

Q76. What is SPT and what corrections are applied to N-value?

13.2 Pile Foundations

Q78. What are static formulas for pile capacity in sand and clay?
Answer: Qu = Qp + Qs. Sand: Qp = Ap.q'.Nq. Clay: Qp = 9.cu.Ap, Qs = alpha.cu.As
Explanation: Ultimate pile capacity = End bearing + Skin friction

In Sand:
End bearing: Qp = Ap x q' x Nq
Skin friction: Qs = K x sigma'v x tan(delta) x As
K = 0.5-1.0, delta = 0.67phi to phi

In Clay:
End bearing: Qp = 9 x cu x Ap (Nc=9 for deep foundations)
Skin friction: Qs = alpha x cu x As (alpha-method)
alpha: cu<25kPa: 1.0 | cu=25-70: decreasing | cu>70: 0.3-0.5

Safe load: Qsafe = Qu/FOS (FOS = 2.5)

Group efficiency:
Clay: eta can be < 1 (block failure check needed)
Sand: eta >= 1 (densification effect)
Sand: Qp=Ap.q'.Nq | Clay: Qp=9cu.Ap, Qs=alpha.cu.As | FOS=2.5

Explanation:

Qp = 0.1257 x 9 x 60 = 67.86 kN

Skin friction: As = pi x 0.4 x 15 = 18.85 m²

Ultimate: Qu = 67.86 + 791.7 = 859.5 kN
Safe: Qsafe = 859.5/2.5 = 344 kN

13.3 Slope Stability

Q80. What are the methods of slope stability analysis?
Answer: Infinite slope: FOS = tan(phi)/tan(alpha). Circular: Swedish method, Bishop's method, Taylor's charts.
Explanation:

1. Infinite Slope:
Dry cohesionless: FOS = tan(phi)/tan(alpha) (independent of depth!)
With seepage: FOS = (gamma'/gamma_sat) x tan(phi)/tan(alpha) = ~0.5 x tan(phi)/tan(alpha)
Cohesive: FOS = c'/(gamma.z.sin(alpha).cos(alpha)) + tan(phi')/tan(alpha)

2. Swedish Circle (Fellenius):
FOS = Sum[c'l + (W.cos(alpha) - ul)tan(phi')] / Sum[W.sin(alpha)]
Ignores inter-slice forces (conservative)

3. Bishop's Simplified:
More accurate, considers horizontal inter-slice forces. Iterative.

4. Taylor's Charts (phi=0, undrained clay):
Stability number Sn = cu/(gamma.Hc)
FOS = Hc/H = cu/(gamma.H.Sn)
For beta < 53 deg: toe circle. For beta > 53 deg: base circle possible.
Infinite: FOS=tan(phi)/tan(alpha) | Taylor: FOS=cu/(gamma.H.Sn) | Min FOS=1.5

Explanation:

Step 1: tan(beta) = 1/1.5 = 0.667, beta = 33.7 deg

Step 2: From Taylor's chart for phi=0, beta=33.7 deg: Sn = 0.17

Step 3: FOS = cu/(gamma x H x Sn) = 40/(18 x 6 x 0.17) = 40/18.36 = 2.18

Min FOS required: 1.5 (temporary) to 2.0 (permanent)
Here FOS = 2.18 > 1.5. Safe.

15.4 Structural Analysis - Most Repeated

Q96. What is degree of static indeterminacy?
Answer: Ds = (3m + r) - 3j (for pin-jointed frames) | Ds = (3m + r) - 3j (rigid frames: different formula)
Explanation:

For beams: Ds = R - 3 (R = number of reactions)
- Simply supported: R=3, Ds=0 (determinate)
- Propped cantilever: R=4, Ds=1
- Fixed beam: R=6, Ds=3
- Continuous beam (n spans): Ds = R - 3

For pin-jointed trusses:
Ds = m + r - 2j
Where: m=members, r=reactions, j=joints
- Perfect frame: m = 2j - 3
- Deficient: m < 2j - 3 (mechanism)
- Redundant: m > 2j - 3 (indeterminate)

For rigid frames:
Ds = 3m + r - 3j (external + internal)
Or: Dse (external) = R - 3, Dsi (internal) = 3 x closed loops

Degree of kinematic indeterminacy (Dk):
Dk = 3j - r (for rigid frames, ignoring axial deformation)
With axial deformation: Dk = 3j - r + number of members
Beam: Ds=R-3 | Truss: Ds=m+r-2j | Frame: Ds=3m+r-3j | Dk=3j-r
Q97. What are the theorems of structural analysis (Castigliano, Maxwell)?
Answer: Castigliano I: dU/dP = deflection. Maxwell: deflection at A due to load at B = deflection at B due to load at A.
Explanation:

Castigliano's First Theorem:
Partial derivative of strain energy w.r.t. a force = displacement in direction of that force
delta = dU/dP

Castigliano's Second Theorem:
dU/dR = 0 for redundant reactions (minimum energy principle)
Used to solve indeterminate structures.

Maxwell's Reciprocal Theorem:
delta_ij = delta_ji
Deflection at point i due to unit load at j = Deflection at j due to unit load at i

Unit Load Method (Virtual Work):
delta = Integral(Mm/EI)dx for beams
delta = Sum(PpL/AE) for trusses
Where: M=real BM, m=virtual BM due to unit load

Betti's Theorem: Generalization of Maxwell's theorem for multiple loads.
Work done by system 1 forces through system 2 displacements = Work done by system 2 forces through system 1 displacements.

15.8 Fluid Mechanics - Most Repeated

Q105. What is the difference between laminar and turbulent flow?
Answer: Laminar: Re<2000, orderly layers, Vmax=2Vavg. Turbulent: Re>4000, chaotic, Vmax=1.2Vavg.
Explanation:

Laminar Flow (Re < 2000):
- Fluid moves in parallel layers (no mixing)
- Velocity profile: parabolic
- Vmax = 2 x Vavg (at center of pipe)
- Friction factor: f = 64/Re
- Head loss proportional to V (first power)
- Shear stress: tau = mu x dv/dy (Newton's law)

Turbulent Flow (Re > 4000):
- Chaotic mixing, eddies
- Velocity profile: flatter (logarithmic)
- Vmax = 1.2 x Vavg approximately
- Friction factor: from Moody diagram
- Head loss proportional to V^2 (approximately)
- Higher energy loss than laminar

Transition zone: 2000 < Re < 4000 (unstable)

Hagen-Poiseuille (laminar only):
Q = pi.d^4.dP/(128.mu.L)
hf = 32.mu.V.L/(rho.g.d^2)
Laminar: Re<2000, f=64/Re, Vmax=2Vavg | Turbulent: Re>4000, Vmax=1.2Vavg

15.10 Environmental Engineering - Most Repeated

Q109. What is hardness of water and how is it removed?
Answer: Temporary (carbonate): removed by boiling. Permanent (non-carbonate): removed by lime-soda/zeolite process.
Explanation:

Temporary hardness (carbonate hardness):
- Due to: Ca(HCO3)2 and Mg(HCO3)2
- Removed by: Boiling (converts to insoluble CaCO3)
- Ca(HCO3)2 --heat--> CaCO3 (ppt) + H2O + CO2

Permanent hardness (non-carbonate):
- Due to: CaSO4, CaCl2, MgSO4, MgCl2
- NOT removed by boiling
- Removed by: Lime-soda process, Zeolite process, Ion exchange

Total hardness = Temporary + Permanent
Expressed as mg/L of CaCO3 equivalent

Lime-Soda Process:
- Lime [Ca(OH)2]: removes temporary hardness + CO2
- Soda ash [Na2CO3]: removes permanent hardness
- Excess lime: 0.5-1.0 mg/L added for complete reaction

Zeolite (Ion Exchange) Process:
- Na2Ze + Ca^2+ --> CaZe + 2Na+ (softening)
- Regeneration: CaZe + NaCl --> Na2Ze + CaCl2
- Removes hardness to nearly zero

IS 10500 limit: 200 mg/L (desirable), 600 mg/L (permissible)

- When sewage is discharged into stream:
- Deoxygenation: bacteria consume DO to decompose organics

- Higher ratio = faster recovery

16.1 SOM - Strain Energy & Impact Loading

Q111. What is strain energy stored in a bar under axial load?
Answer: U = P^2.L/(2AE) = sigma^2.V/(2E) = P.delta/2
Explanation:

Strain energy: Energy stored in a body due to deformation.

U = (1/2) x P x delta = P^2.L/(2AE) = sigma^2.V/(2E)

Where: P=load, delta=deformation, L=length, A=area, E=modulus, V=volume

Resilience: Strain energy per unit volume at elastic limit
u = sigma_e^2/(2E)

Proof resilience: Max strain energy stored without permanent deformation
= sigma_y^2 x V/(2E)

Modulus of resilience: Proof resilience per unit volume = sigma_y^2/(2E)

For shear: U = tau^2.V/(2G)
For bending: U = Integral[M^2/(2EI)]dx
For torsion: U = T^2.L/(2GJ)

Impact loading:
sigma_impact = sigma_static x [1 + sqrt(1 + 2h/delta_st)]
Where h = height of drop, delta_st = static deflection
For h=0 (sudden): sigma = 2 x sigma_static
U = P^2L/2AE = sigma^2.V/2E | Impact: sigma = sigma_st[1+sqrt(1+2h/delta_st)]

Explanation:

Strain energy in bending: U = Integral[M^2/(2EI)]dx

M(x) = Px/2 for 0 to L/2
U = 2 x Integral_0^(L/2) [(Px/2)^2/(2EI)]dx
= 2 x P^2/(8EI) x [x^3/3]_0^(L/2)
= P^2.L^3/(96EI)

SS beam with UDL w:
M(x) = wLx/2 - wx^2/2
U = Integral_0^L [M^2/(2EI)]dx = w^2.L^5/(240EI)

Cantilever with end load P:
U = P^2.L^3/(6EI)

Cantilever with UDL w:
U = w^2.L^5/(40EI)

delta = dU/dP gives deflection at point of load

16.6 Hydrology - Repeated Concepts

Q121. What is a unit hydrograph and its assumptions?
Answer: UH = hydrograph of 1 cm direct runoff from a rainfall of specific duration over entire catchment.
Explanation:

Definition: A unit hydrograph is the direct runoff hydrograph (DRH) resulting from 1 unit (1 cm) of effective rainfall occurring uniformly over the catchment in a specified duration.

Assumptions:
1. Linearity: Ordinates of DRH are proportional to rainfall excess
(2 cm rainfall gives 2x the ordinates)
2. Superposition: DRH from multiple periods can be added
3. Time invariance: UH doesn't change with time (same UH applies always)
4. Uniform rainfall distribution over entire catchment
5. Uniform rainfall intensity during the specified duration
6. Base time is constant regardless of rainfall intensity

Applications:
- Flood estimation for ungauged catchments
- Design of spillways, bridges, culverts
- Flood forecasting

Limitations:
- Valid only for catchment area < 5000 km^2
- Assumes linear response (not valid for extreme events)
- Doesn't account for non-uniform rainfall

S-curve: Used to derive UH of different duration from given UH.
Q122. What is a flood frequency analysis and return period?
Answer: Return period T = 1/P (P=probability of exceedance). Gumbel: xT = x_mean + K.sigma. Log-Pearson Type III also used.
Explanation:

Return period (T): Average time interval between occurrences of an event of given magnitude.
T = 1/P where P = probability of exceedance in any year

Risk (R): Probability of event occurring at least once in n years:
R = 1 - (1-1/T)^n

Gumbel's Method (Extreme Value Type I):
xT = x_mean + K x sigma
K = (yT - yn)/Sn (frequency factor)
yT = -ln[-ln(1-1/T)] (reduced variate)
yn, Sn = mean and std dev of reduced variate (from tables, depend on n)

Log-Pearson Type III:
log(xT) = log(x)_mean + K x sigma_log
K depends on skewness coefficient and T

Plotting position (Weibull):
P = m/(N+1) where m = rank (descending order), N = total years
T = (N+1)/m

Design flood:
- Spillway: PMF (Probable Maximum Flood) or 10,000 year flood
- Bridges: 50-100 year flood
- Culverts: 25-50 year flood
T = 1/P | Risk R = 1-(1-1/T)^n | Gumbel: xT = x_mean + K.sigma | Weibull: P = m/(N+1)

Q123. What is CPM and PERT? What are the differences?
Answer: CPM: deterministic (single time estimate), activity-oriented. PERT: probabilistic (3 time estimates), event-oriented.
Explanation:

CPM (Critical Path Method):
- Deterministic: single time estimate per activity
- Activity-oriented (focuses on activities)
- Used for repetitive/known projects (construction)
- Cost optimization possible (crashing)
- Critical path = longest path = minimum project duration

PERT (Program Evaluation Review Technique):
- Probabilistic: 3 time estimates per activity
te = (to + 4tm + tp)/6 (expected time)
Variance = [(tp - to)/6]^2
- Event-oriented (focuses on milestones)
- Used for R&D/new projects (uncertain durations)
- No cost optimization

Common terms:
- EST (Earliest Start Time): forward pass
- EFT = EST + duration
- LST (Latest Start Time): backward pass
- LFT = LST + duration
- Total Float = LST - EST = LFT - EFT
- Free Float = EST(next) - EFT(current)
- Critical activity: Total Float = 0

Project duration variance: Sum of variances on critical path
P(completion in T) = P(Z) where Z = (T - Te)/sqrt(sum of variances)
te = (to+4tm+tp)/6 | Variance = [(tp-to)/6]^2 | Float = LST-EST | Critical: Float=0
Q124. What is crashing in CPM and how to find optimum project duration?
Answer: Crashing = reducing activity duration by adding resources. Crash critical activities with lowest cost slope first.
Explanation:

Crashing: Reducing project duration by allocating additional resources (at extra cost).

Cost slope: = (Crash cost - Normal cost) / (Normal time - Crash time)
Units: Rs per day saved

Procedure:
1. Find critical path and its duration
2. Calculate cost slope for each critical activity
3. Crash the activity with LOWEST cost slope first
4. Continue until: all critical activities are at crash time, OR
indirect cost savings < direct cost increase

Total project cost = Direct cost + Indirect cost
- Direct cost increases with crashing
- Indirect cost decreases with shorter duration (overheads, rent)
- Optimum duration: where total cost is minimum

Important:
- Only crash CRITICAL activities (non-critical crashing is waste)
- When multiple critical paths exist, crash common activity or crash one from each path
- Activity cannot be crashed below its crash duration

Key concept: Copper expands MORE than steel. Since they are bonded:
- Copper is RESTRAINED (compressed)
- Steel is PULLED (tensioned)

18.2 Fluid Mechanics - More Key Concepts

Q138. What is dimensional analysis and Buckingham Pi theorem?
Answer: Pi theorem: if n variables involve m fundamental dimensions, then (n-m) dimensionless pi terms exist.
Explanation:

Buckingham Pi Theorem:
If a phenomenon involves n variables and m fundamental dimensions (M,L,T),
then it can be expressed as (n-m) independent dimensionless groups (pi terms).

Procedure:
1. List all n variables affecting the phenomenon
2. Identify m fundamental dimensions (usually M, L, T = 3)
3. Select m repeating variables (must contain all dimensions)
4. Form (n-m) pi terms using repeating variables + one other variable each
5. Express relationship: f(pi1, pi2, ...) = 0

Important dimensionless numbers:
- Reynolds (Re) = rho.V.L/mu = Inertia/Viscous (pipe flow, drag)
- Froude (Fr) = V/sqrt(gL) = Inertia/Gravity (open channel, ships)
- Euler (Eu) = P/(rho.V^2) = Pressure/Inertia (cavitation)
- Mach (Ma) = V/c = Inertia/Elastic (compressible flow)
- Weber (We) = rho.V^2.L/sigma = Inertia/Surface tension (droplets)

Model laws:
- Reynolds model law: pipe flow, submarine, aircraft
- Froude model law: open channel, spillway, weir, ship
- Scale ratios derived from respective similarity
n variables, m dimensions = (n-m) pi terms | Re=rhoVL/mu | Fr=V/sqrt(gL)

19.2 Advanced Soil Mechanics

Q149. What is Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion and how to find shear strength parameters from triaxial test?
Answer: tau_f = c + sigma_n.tan(phi). From Mohr circle: sin(phi) = (sigma1-sigma3)/(sigma1+sigma3) for c=0.
Explanation:

Mohr-Coulomb criterion:
tau_f = c + sigma_n x tan(phi)

From triaxial test results:
- Plot Mohr circles for each test (sigma3, sigma1)
- Draw common tangent = failure envelope
- Intercept on tau axis = c (cohesion)
- Slope of envelope = phi (friction angle)

For cohesionless soil (c=0):
sin(phi) = (sigma1-sigma3)/(sigma1+sigma3)
sigma1 = sigma3 x tan^2(45+phi/2) = sigma3 x Kp

For purely cohesive soil (phi=0):
cu = (sigma1-sigma3)/2
qu = sigma1 - sigma3 = 2cu (unconfined compressive strength)

Relationship between principal stresses at failure:
sigma1 = sigma3.tan^2(45+phi/2) + 2c.tan(45+phi/2)
sigma1 = sigma3.Kp + 2c.sqrt(Kp)

Failure plane angle:
theta = 45 + phi/2 (from major principal plane)

Effective stress parameters:
Use sigma' = sigma - u (pore pressure) for drained analysis
tau_f = c+sigma.tan(phi) | sin(phi)=(s1-s3)/(s1+s3) | Failure plane: 45+phi/2 | qu=2cu
Q150. What is the difference between total stress and effective stress analysis?
Answer: sigma' = sigma - u (Terzaghi). Total stress: uses cu, phi_u. Effective stress: uses c', phi' (more fundamental).
Explanation:

Terzaghi's Effective Stress Principle:
sigma' = sigma - u
Where: sigma'=effective stress, sigma=total stress, u=pore water pressure

Key points:
- Shear strength depends on EFFECTIVE stress only
- Compressibility depends on effective stress
- All measurable effects are due to effective stress changes

Total Stress Analysis (TSA):
- Uses undrained parameters: cu, phi_u
- For phi_u=0 (saturated clay): tau_f = cu
- Quick/short-term stability (during construction)
- No need to know pore pressure
- UU test parameters used

Effective Stress Analysis (ESA):
- Uses drained parameters: c', phi'
- tau_f = c' + sigma'.tan(phi')
- Long-term stability (after consolidation)
- Need to know/estimate pore pressure
- CD or CU (with pore pressure) test parameters

When to use which:
- End of construction (clay): TSA (phi=0)
- Long-term (any soil): ESA
- Sand (always drained): ESA
- Rapid drawdown: TSA

For sand: c'=0, phi'=30-45 deg. Always use ESA.

Q151. What is the design of gravity dam? What are the stability checks?

Explanation:

Q157. What are admixtures in concrete and their types?
Answer: Plasticizers (reduce water 10%), Superplasticizers (reduce 20-30%), Retarders (delay setting), Accelerators (speed up).
Explanation:

Chemical Admixtures (IS 9103):

1. Plasticizers (water reducers):
- Reduce water by 10-15% for same workability
- OR increase workability for same w/c
- Types: lignosulphonates, hydroxylated carboxylic acids
- Dosage: 0.1-0.5% by weight of cement

2. Superplasticizers (high range water reducers):
- Reduce water by 20-30%
- Used for: high strength concrete, SCC, pumped concrete
- Types: SNF, SMF, polycarboxylate ethers (PCE)
- Dosage: 0.5-2.0% by weight of cement

3. Retarders:
- Delay initial setting time
- Use: hot weather, long transport, mass concrete
- Types: sugar, calcium lignosulphonate

4. Accelerators:
- Speed up setting and early strength
- Use: cold weather, urgent repairs
- Types: CaCl2 (max 2%), triethanolamine
- CaCl2 NOT for prestressed/reinforced concrete (causes corrosion)

5. Air-entraining agents:
- Introduce tiny air bubbles (3-6% air)
- Improves: frost resistance, workability
- Reduces: strength (5% per 1% air), permeability

Mineral admixtures: Fly ash, GGBS, silica fume, metakaolin
Q158. What is creep and shrinkage in concrete?
Answer: Creep: time-dependent deformation under sustained load. Shrinkage: volume decrease without load (drying). Both cause long-term deflection.
Explanation:

Creep:
- Continuous deformation under constant sustained stress
- Creep strain = 1 to 3 times elastic strain (creep coefficient)
- IS 456: ultimate creep coefficient = 1.6 (7 days loading), 1.1 (1 year)
- Factors increasing creep: higher stress, higher w/c, higher temperature, loading at early age, smaller member size

Effects of creep:
- Increased deflection in beams (long-term deflection = 2-3 x initial)
- Loss of prestress in PSC (15-20%)
- Redistribution of stresses in columns
- Relaxation of stress in restrained members

Shrinkage:
- Drying shrinkage: loss of moisture to atmosphere
- Autogenous shrinkage: self-desiccation during hydration
- Plastic shrinkage: before setting (causes surface cracks)
- IS 456: ultimate shrinkage strain = 0.0003

Effects of shrinkage:
- Cracking (if restrained)
- Warping of slabs
- Loss of prestress

Control measures:
- Low w/c ratio, proper curing
- Use of shrinkage-compensating cement
- Provide contraction joints
- Minimum reinforcement for crack control

20.2 Prestressed Concrete

Q162. What is prestressed concrete and what are the methods of prestressing?
Answer: Pre-tensioning: stress before casting (factory). Post-tensioning: stress after casting (site). Eliminates tension in concrete.
Explanation:

Principle: Apply compressive stress to concrete before loading, so that under service loads, the net stress remains compressive (no cracking).

Pre-tensioning:
- Tendons stressed BEFORE concrete is cast
- Concrete cast around stressed tendons
- After hardening, tendons released - prestress transferred by BOND
- Used for: precast members (beams, slabs, poles, sleepers)
- Factory production, standard sections

Post-tensioning:
- Concrete cast with ducts/sheaths for tendons
- After concrete hardens, tendons stressed using jacks
- Prestress transferred through ANCHORAGES at ends
- Ducts grouted after stressing (bonded) or left ungrouted (unbonded)
- Used for: large spans, bridges, in-situ construction

Advantages of PSC over RCC:
- No cracks under service loads (better durability)
- Smaller sections (less dead weight)
- Longer spans possible (30-50m vs 10-15m for RCC)
- Better fatigue resistance
- Reduced deflections

Materials:
- Concrete: min M40 (high strength needed)
- Steel: high tensile (fpu = 1500-1860 MPa) - wires, strands, bars
- HTS wire: 5-7mm dia, stress 1000-1400 MPa
Q163. What are the losses of prestress?
Answer: Immediate: elastic shortening, friction, anchorage slip. Time-dependent: creep, shrinkage, relaxation. Total loss: 15-25%.
Explanation:

Immediate losses (at transfer):

1. Elastic shortening:
- Concrete shortens when prestress applied
- Pre-tensioning: loss = m.fc (m=Es/Ec, fc=stress at CG of steel)
- Post-tensioning (sequential): loss = 0.5.m.fc (average)

2. Friction (post-tensioning only):
- Between tendon and duct
- Px = Po.e^-(mu.alpha + k.x)
- mu = curvature friction (0.25-0.55)
- k = wobble coefficient (0.0015-0.005 per m)

3. Anchorage slip (post-tensioning):
- Slip at wedge anchorage: 2-6mm
- Loss = delta.Es/L (significant for short members)

Time-dependent losses:

4. Creep of concrete:
- Loss = m x fc x creep coefficient (phi)
- phi = 1.6 (loading at 7 days)
- Typically 5-7% of initial prestress

5. Shrinkage of concrete:
- Loss = epsilon_sh x Es
- epsilon_sh = 0.0003 (IS 1343)
- Loss = 0.0003 x 200000 = 60 MPa typically

6. Relaxation of steel:
- Stress reduces at constant strain over time
- 2-8% of initial stress (depends on steel type)

Total losses: 15-25% of initial prestress
Pre-tensioning: 18-22% | Post-tensioning: 15-20%
Elastic shortening: m.fc | Friction: Px=Po.e^-(mu.a+kx) | Shrinkage: eps.Es | Total: 15-25%

20.4 Advanced Hydraulics

Q165. What is water hammer in pipes and how to prevent it?
Answer: Pressure rise dp = rho.c.dV where c = sqrt(K/rho) for rigid pipe. Prevention: slow valve closure, surge tanks, air vessels.
Explanation:

Water hammer: Sudden pressure rise/drop in pipe due to rapid change in velocity (valve closure/opening).

Pressure rise (rigid pipe):
dp = rho x c x dV
c = sqrt(K/rho) = speed of pressure wave
For water: K = 2.1 GPa, c = 1440 m/s

For elastic pipe:
c = sqrt(K/rho) / sqrt(1 + K.D/(E.t))
Where: D=pipe diameter, t=wall thickness, E=pipe material modulus

Critical time:
Tc = 2L/c (time for wave to travel to reservoir and back)
- If valve closure time T < Tc: rapid closure (full water hammer)
- If T > Tc: slow closure (reduced pressure rise)

For slow closure:
dp = rho x L x V / T (much less than rapid closure)

Prevention methods:
1. Slow valve closure (T >> Tc)
2. Surge tanks (open tanks near valve - absorb pressure)
3. Air vessels (closed tanks with compressed air)
4. Pressure relief valves
5. Flywheel on pumps (gradual deceleration)

Surge tank types: Simple, restricted orifice, differential
dp = rho.c.dV | c = sqrt(K/rho) = 1440 m/s (water) | Tc = 2L/c | Slow: dp = rho.L.V/T
Q166. What are the types of turbines and their specific speeds?

Explanation:

Classification by energy conversion:

1. Impulse turbine (Pelton wheel):
- All energy converted to KE before runner
- Pressure at runner = atmospheric
- High head (150-2000m), low discharge
- Ns = 10-35 (per jet)
- Bucket-shaped vanes, horizontal/vertical shaft
- Efficiency: 85-90%

2. Reaction turbine - Francis:
- Both pressure and velocity change in runner
- Runner fully submerged (enclosed)
- Medium head (30-500m), medium discharge
- Ns = 60-300
- Mixed flow (radial inlet, axial outlet)
- Efficiency: 90-95%

3. Reaction turbine - Kaplan (propeller):
- Axial flow, adjustable blades
- Low head (5-80m), high discharge
- Ns = 300-900
- Efficiency: 90-93%

Ns = N.sqrt(P) / H^(5/4) [N in rpm, P in kW, H in m]

- High head, low Q: Pelton

- Low head, high Q: Kaplan

Q167. What are the conditions of equilibrium and types of supports?

Explanation:

Conditions of equilibrium (2D):
1. Sum Fx = 0 (no net horizontal force)
2. Sum Fy = 0 (no net vertical force)
3. Sum M = 0 (no net moment about any point)

Types of supports and reactions:

1. Roller support:
- 1 reaction (perpendicular to surface)
- Allows: translation along surface, rotation
- Restrains: translation perpendicular to surface

2. Hinge (pin) support:
- 2 reactions (horizontal + vertical)
- Allows: rotation
- Restrains: translation in both directions

3. Fixed support:
- 3 reactions (horizontal + vertical + moment)
- Allows: nothing
- Restrains: all movements

Determinacy:
- Statically determinate: R = 3 (can solve with equilibrium alone)
- Indeterminate: R > 3 (need compatibility equations)
- Mechanism: R < 3 (unstable)

- Isolate body, show all external forces and reactions
- Essential first step in any structural analysis

Q173. What is boundary layer theory and its parameters?
Answer: delta (thickness where u=0.99U). Laminar BL: delta=5x/sqrt(Rex). Turbulent: delta=0.37x/Rex^(1/5).
Explanation:

Boundary layer: Thin layer near solid surface where viscous effects are significant.

Laminar boundary layer (Rex < 5x10^5):
- delta = 5x / sqrt(Rex) [Blasius solution]
- delta* (displacement thickness) = 1.73x/sqrt(Rex)
- theta (momentum thickness) = 0.664x/sqrt(Rex)
- Cf (local friction) = 0.664/sqrt(Rex)
- CD (average drag) = 1.328/sqrt(ReL)

Turbulent boundary layer (Rex > 5x10^5):
- delta = 0.37x / Rex^(1/5)
- Cf = 0.0592 / Rex^(1/5)
- CD = 0.074 / ReL^(1/5)

Boundary layer separation:
- Occurs when dp/dx > 0 (adverse pressure gradient)
- At separation: (du/dy) at wall = 0
- Causes: increased drag, wake formation
- Prevention: streamlining, suction, blowing

Displacement thickness (delta*):
= integral_0^delta (1 - u/U) dy
Physical meaning: distance by which streamlines shift outward

Momentum thickness (theta):
= integral_0^delta (u/U)(1 - u/U) dy
Related to drag force on plate
Laminar: delta=5x/sqrt(Rex) | Turbulent: delta=0.37x/Rex^0.2 | Separation: du/dy=0 at wall
Q174. What is the difference between pipe flow in series and parallel?
Answer: Series: same Q, head losses add. Parallel: same head loss, discharges add.
Explanation:

Pipes in Series:
- Same discharge through all pipes: Q1 = Q2 = Q3 = Q
- Total head loss = sum of individual losses: hf = hf1 + hf2 + hf3
- Using Darcy: hf = f.L.V^2/(2gD) for each pipe
- Equivalent pipe: same hf and Q with single pipe
f.Le/De^5 = f1.L1/D1^5 + f2.L2/D2^5 + ... (if same f)

Pipes in Parallel:
- Same head loss across all pipes: hf1 = hf2 = hf3
- Total discharge = sum: Q = Q1 + Q2 + Q3
- Each pipe has same pressure drop between junctions

Equivalent pipe (Dupuit's equation):
For same f: L/D^5 = L1/D1^5 + L2/D2^5 (series)
For parallel: D^(5/2)/sqrt(L) = D1^(5/2)/sqrt(L1) + D2^(5/2)/sqrt(L2)

Hardy-Cross method:
- For pipe networks (loops)
- Iterative: assume flows, correct using delta_Q = -Sum(hf)/Sum(2hf/Q)
- Continuity at each node: Sum(Q_in) = Sum(Q_out)
- Around each loop: Sum(hf) = 0

Three-reservoir problem:
- Find piezometric head at junction
- Assume direction of flow, solve iteratively
Series: Q same, hf adds | Parallel: hf same, Q adds | Hardy-Cross: dQ = -Sum(hf)/Sum(2hf/Q)

Q175. What is the coefficient of consolidation and how to determine it?

Where: k=permeability, mv=coefficient of volume compressibility
Units: m^2/year or cm^2/sec

- Plot settlement vs log(t)
- Find t50 from intersection of tangents
- Cv = 0.197 x d^2 / t50

t_field = Tv x d_field^2 / Cv

Q176. What is the Swedish circle method for slope stability in detail?
Answer: FOS = Sum[c'.l + (W.cos(alpha)-u.l).tan(phi')] / Sum[W.sin(alpha)]. Divide into 5-10 slices.
Explanation: Method of Slices (Fellenius/Swedish):

Procedure:
1. Assume a trial circular failure surface (center O, radius R)
2. Divide soil mass into vertical slices (5-10 slices)
3. For each slice, determine: W (weight), alpha (base angle), l (base length), u (pore pressure)

Factor of Safety:
FOS = Sum[c'.l + (W.cos(alpha) - u.l).tan(phi')] / Sum[W.sin(alpha)]

= Sum(Resisting forces) / Sum(Driving forces)

For each slice:
- W = gamma x b x h (weight = unit weight x width x avg height)
- l = b / cos(alpha) (base length)
- Normal force N = W.cos(alpha)
- Tangential force T = W.sin(alpha)
- Pore pressure force U = u x l

For phi=0 analysis (total stress):
FOS = Sum(cu.l.R) / Sum(W.x)
= cu x R x arc length / Sum(W.x)
Where x = moment arm of W about center O

Finding critical circle:
- Try multiple circles with different centers
- Minimum FOS = critical FOS
- Fellenius suggested locus of centers for critical circle

Limitations: Ignores inter-slice forces (conservative by 5-15%)
